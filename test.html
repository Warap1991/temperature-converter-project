Théorie sur les unités de mesure de la température

Kelvin (K), Celsius (°C), and Fahrenheit (°F) are three commonly used units of temperature measurement. Each unit has its own scale and reference points, and they are used in different regions of the world. Here's an explanation of the differences between these temperature units:

  1. Kelvin (K):The Kelvin is an absolute temperature scale which starts from absolute zero, which is the point at which all molecular motion ceases. Absolute zero is approximately -273.15°C or -459.67°F.
  2. Celsius (°C):The Celsius scale, also known as the centigrade scale, is based on the freezing and boiling points of water at standard atmospheric pressure. 0°C is the freezing point of water, and 100°C is the boiling point of water at sea level.The conversion formula between Celsius and Kelvin is: Kelvin = Celsius + 273.15
  3. Fahrenheit (°F):The Fahrenheit scale is based on the freezing and boiling points of water, but it uses different reference points than Celsius. 32°F is the freezing point of water, and 212°F is the boiling point of water at standard atmospheric pressure.

The conversion formula between Fahrenheit and Celsius is: Celsius = (Fahrenheit - 32) × 5/9. The conversion formula between Fahrenheit and Kelvin is more complex: Kelvin = (Fahrenheit + 459.67) × 5/9.
